Logotype Assignment

Assignment Description:

• Use typefaces to create a logotype that integrates the initials of your first and last names (two letters) into a unified whole.
• The letters should be based initially on these five typefaces: Garamond, Baskerville, Bodoni, Century Expanded and Helvetica.
• The lettermark must contain curves and the letters must “read” in consecutive order: first initial first, then the last initial.

Process

Brainstorming


“Design dumping” in my sketchbook, not just really ugly drawings. Playing around with the basic interactivity between the letters, shading, and the connotations associated with each typeface. Starred a few of the ideas that I liked at the time.

Drafting


Three variations of the design I was most interested in at the time, an “S” whose top half could also be read as a “C” (#39).
Top row: I tried to freehand the design without tracing the typography first – it came out a lot differently than I imagined it to, but I ended up somehow liking it for some edgy, artsy reason, I guess.
Middle row: Combined the Helvetica “C” with the lower half of an “S”; added some curved edges to create more flow. Played around a lot with different color schemes to find what could draw the eye to the “C” first, then to the overall “S”.
Bottom row: Vertically condensed the “C” to create a more proportional-looking “S”. Continued to play with different colors, though I am apparently quite indecisive.


A variation on design #3 using the intersection of the serifs in the Baskerville typeface. I really just wanted to give the serif font a last chance.


Color variations of the previous design. I wasn’t sure if coloring the “S” a different color without its serif looked too weird or not. I definitely like the gold “S” encircled by a more pastel color – it makes me feel like royalty.


Based on design #25. Halfway through making this, I realized it kind of looked like an egg, so that explains the first color scheme. Because of the typeface limitation on this assignment, it would have been incredibly time consuming to fit the “S” exactly in the “C”, which probably would have made the design look more cohesive and polished. I definitely want to make a completed logo on this design in the future simply because I love eggs.


Based on designs #23, #36-#38. Not much to say besides a simple connection of the “C” and the “S”.


Once again trying to figure out what color I like. I stayed away from using more than one color because it would have created a split in the otherwise cohesive design.

Editing

I decided on the last draft because I think the squiggly line silhouette represents my design ideals and personality the best (from now on I’ll refer to it as “The Squiggly Design”, or the SD for short).


Cut off the end of the “C” and the “S” to open up the design a little more.


Tried to see if I liked having curved edges to enhance the flow of the design – nope.


Back to the straight edges. Shrank the “C” to enhance the curvature; tilted the “S” more to the right to open up more of the negative space and have the overall logo look more “squiggly”.


Rounded the edges to give a more childlike quality to the design, because I am a child.

Conclusion

For the color, I went with “New York Pink” (#e08283) because I felt like it was a fairly mature tone with some playful elements in it. I also rotated the design to make it a little more readable.

Overall, this project allowed me to explore my creativity while working with limited resources (picking from 5 typefaces). It pushed me to brainstorm a lot harder than I would have otherwise had I been able to work with a greater variety of typefaces. The assignment also made me think about my design style, personality, and how I wanted to be reflected by this logo for myself. I had to not only consider how the design looked on the web, but also on print, in different sizes, and within various themes and color schemes. I now have a good idea of how I want to move forward with my designs and personal branding. Finally.

  • Tools used: Adobe Illustrator
  • Date: February 2017
